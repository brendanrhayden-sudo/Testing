Scapular Dyskinesis Assessment Battery

Complete evidence-based testing protocol for scapular movement dysfunction

1. McClure Scapular Dyskinesis Test

Setup & Execution:

  • Position: Patient standing, 3-5 lb weights in hands
  • Movement:
    • Flexion Test: Arms forward to overhead (5 reps)
    • Abduction Test: Arms to side overhead (5 reps)
    • Tempo: 3 seconds up, 3 seconds down
  • Observation: Examiner observes from behind

Grading System:

  • Normal: Smooth, symmetrical movement
  • Subtle Dyskinesis: Slight winging (<1cm), mild dysrhythmia
  • Obvious Dyskinesis: Clear winging (>1cm), marked dysrhythmia
  • Severe: Gross winging, inability to elevate

Pattern Classification (Kibler/Sciascia):

  • Pattern 1: Inferior medial border prominence
  • Pattern 2: Medial border prominence
  • Pattern 3: Superior border elevation
  • Pattern 4: Symmetric dysfunction
Sensitivity: 75% | Specificity: 80%

2. Scapular Assistance Test (SAT)

Purpose:

Differentiate weak serratus anterior from inhibited serratus anterior

Execution:

  1. Patient performs painful arc movement
  2. Examiner manually assists upward rotation and posterior tilt
  3. Place one hand on inferior angle, one on superior aspect
  4. Assist movement through arc

Interpretation:

  • Positive SAT: Pain decreases with assistance = serratus inhibition
  • Negative SAT: No change = true weakness or other pathology
Clinical Value: High for treatment planning

3. Scapular Retraction Test (SRT)

Purpose:

Assess impact of scapular position on shoulder strength

Execution:

  1. Test shoulder elevation strength at 90Â° scaption
  2. Manually retract and stabilize scapula
  3. Retest strength in stabilized position

Interpretation:

  • Positive: >20% strength increase with stabilization
  • Indicates scapular dyskinesis contributing to weakness
Clinical correlation: 85%

4. Lateral Scapular Slide Test (LSST)

Positions & Measurements:

  1. Position 1: Arms at side
    • Measure from inferior angle to spinous process
  2. Position 2: Hands on hips (45Â° abduction)
    • Same measurement
  3. Position 3: Arms at 90Â° abduction, max internal rotation
    • Same measurement

Interpretation:

  • Side-to-side difference >1.5cm = significant asymmetry
  • Document all three positions for pattern recognition
Reliability: ICC 0.75-0.85

5. Muscle Length Assessments

Pectoralis Minor Length Test:

  • Setup: Supine, arm at side
  • Measurement: Distance from table to posterior acromion
  • Interpretation: >2.5cm = shortened pec minor

Levator Scapulae Length Test:

  • Setup: Seated, stabilize scapula in depression
  • Movement: Rotate head away, flex forward
  • Positive: Reproduction of upper trap/neck symptoms

AC Joint Assessment Battery

Evidence-based tests for acromioclavicular joint pathology

1. Cross-Body Adduction Test (Chronopoulos)

Execution:

  • Position: Patient seated or standing
  • Movement: Passive horizontal adduction across body
  • Positive: Pain at AC joint line

Clinical Value:

  • Combined with AC joint tenderness
  • Best single test for AC pathology
Sensitivity: 77% | Specificity: 79%

2. O'Brien's Test (Modified for AC)

Execution:

  • Position: 90Â° forward flexion, 10Â° adduction

3. Paxinos Test

Execution:

  • Position: Patient seated, arm relaxed
  • Test: Examiner applies anterosuperior pressure to mid-clavicle
  • Simultaneously: Apply posterosuperior pressure to spine of scapula
  • Positive: Pain at AC joint

Clinical Note:

Excellent for differentiating AC from subacromial pathology

Sensitivity: 79% | Specificity: 89%

4. AC Resisted Extension Test

Execution:

  • Position: Prone, arm at 90Â° abduction

5. Active Compression Test (Mazzocca)

Purpose:

Determine Type IIIA vs IIIB AC separation

Execution:

  • Patient actively shrugs shoulders
  • Observe clavicle reduction
  • Type IIIA: Reduces with muscle activation
  • Type IIIB: No reduction (surgical consideration)
